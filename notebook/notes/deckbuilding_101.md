# Deckbuilding 101

## Core Concepts & Origins
*   **Deckbuilding as the Game:** The primary innovation of *Dominion*, inspired by games like *Magic: The Gathering*, was to make the act of constructing a deck the central focus of the game itself, rather than a pre-game activity.
*   **The Core Loop:** Players start with a small, weak deck and, on their turn, use cards from their hand to "buy" new, more powerful cards from a shared market. These new cards are added to their discard pile and are eventually shuffled back into their deck, making it progressively stronger.
*   **Player-Driven Randomness:** Deckbuilding gives players a high degree of agency over their own input randomness. You choose which cards (the "pips on the die") to add to your deck, so you own both your lucky combos and your unlucky hands.

## Key Mechanics & Strategies
*   **Engine Building:** The deck *is* the engine. The goal is to build a reliable and efficient deck that allows you to generate resources (money, actions) to buy better cards.
*   **Trashing:** A critical mechanism for improving deck efficiency. It involves removing weak starting cards (like coppers and estates in *Dominion*) from your deck to increase the probability of drawing your more powerful, purchased cards.
*   **The Pivot:** A key strategic element is the timing of "the pivot"—when to stop building your engine and start buying victory point cards. Buying these points is necessary to win but ironically clogs your deck with "junk" cards that have no function, making your engine less efficient and creating a tense endgame race.
*   **Tempo and Combos:** The game is often a race to build the most efficient engine first. A core joy of the genre is discovering and executing powerful card combinations ("combos") that create explosive turns, such as drawing many extra cards or taking multiple actions.

## Player Experience & Game Design
*   **High Feedback Loop:** Deckbuilding games provide constant, direct feedback. A poorly drawn hand or a sputtering engine immediately signals that your previous decisions might have been suboptimal, making it easier to learn and understand why you are winning or losing.
*   **Market Variations:**
    *   **Static Market (Dominion-style):** A fixed set of 10 available cards creates a unique strategic "puzzle" to solve for each game.
    *   **River Market (Ascension-style):** A constantly refreshing row of cards drawn from a larger deck, which makes the game more tactical and opportunistic. This has become the more common style in modern deckbuilders.
*   **Adaptability & Popularity:** The mechanic is highly adaptable to different themes (sci-fi, fantasy, historical) and can be integrated with other mechanics (like board movement in *Clank*). Its popularity stems from this flexibility, its satisfying "junk food" feel of pulling off combos, and its frictionless approach to engine building.
