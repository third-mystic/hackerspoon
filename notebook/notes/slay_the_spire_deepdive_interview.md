# Slay The Spire – A Designers Deep Dive with Anthony Giovannetti

**Audio Transcription: The Game Design Roundtable - Episode 211**

**Speakers:** Dirk Knemeyer, David Heron, and Anthony Giovannetti (Co-creator of *Slay the Spire*).

---

**[00:00] Dirk Knemeyer:** Greetings listeners, and welcome to episode 211 of The Game Design Roundtable. Dirk Knemeyer and David Heron joined this week by Anthony Giovannetti, the co-creator of hit indie digital game, *Slay the Spire*. In fact, today is all about *Slay the Spire*, as we take a designer’s deep dive into this unique and engaging game. Let’s get started.

**[00:24] David Heron:** Dungeon crawler is perhaps the most common theme in all of gaming. Why did you choose it for your game?

**[00:31] Anthony Giovannetti:** The dungeon crawl theme was actually kind of baked in from the beginning. Maybe because it’s such a common theme, it was just kind of a baseline assumption. Originally, I was just thinking, "Okay, well I want to combine roguelikes and deck-building games together," and when I think of a traditional roguelike, I just think of dungeon crawling. It’s kind of a baked-in, built-in assumption. And when I was creating the initial design document, kind of like the first line was just *Darkest Dungeon* plus *Dominion*. And it was like that. So, yeah, something like that was just there from the beginning, and I never had like a real, you know, design reason behind it so much as it was just there from the start and I didn't have a reason to really challenge it.

**[01:14] Dirk Knemeyer:** You mentioned combining genres. I think *Slay the Spire* actually combines a bunch of different genres—you know, roguelike, deck-building, tactical combat, among others. Talk about the influences other than the couple you've mentioned already for the game that culminated in such a unique mix of characteristics.

**[01:31] Anthony Giovannetti:** Originally, I actually come from a background where in my past I managed a game store, so I played all kinds of deck-building games. I was a huge fan of the genre. And I’m also just a big fan of roguelikes; played a lot of *Spelunky*, anything new in roguelike I’ve loved. So I felt that those two genres in particular would combine mechanically very well because a roguelike would give you a good framework and a good reason behind wanting to build a new deck every time you played. And then the kind of tactical combat was just a result of how I saw that framework making sense together. So that was more of like a, "Okay, well what are you going to be doing with your cards?" Combat is clearly the correct choice. And then, "How can I make as rich of a combat as possible?" and then that flowed from that, if that makes sense.

**[02:22] David Heron:** Yeah, yeah, it does. Bringing—it's so interesting that you worked at a game store and you've played all these games. When I was working on *Legend of the Five Rings*, I spent a lot of time at a lot of different stores and met a bunch of different people. And one of the things that sort of came up that was always a sticking point were like these combo control decks. And typically the combo control decks often relied on mechanics like recursive elements—so like, you get to play a game, you play a card, then you can bounce that card back to your hand and play it again, and trying to create, you know, infinite combos and stuff like that, often through card draw. What’s interesting is like, those things were kind of reviled and we had to sort of really balance where do we add these elements for the players that like playing those things versus when we didn’t. *Slay the Spire* like really leans into—like you have a lot of card draw, a lot of ways of thinning decks, a lot of almost infinite-style bouncing things back to your deck. Some of the characters, like, that’s what they do. Where did that like come from?

**[03:22] Anthony Giovannetti:** Well, a lot of that comes from just the fact that we’re a single-player game. So—I also, you know, I played a ton of *Magic* from when I was like a little kid growing up and other card games like *Netrunner* and stuff. And what I found is that playing those combo decks can actually be very fun. Like, you know, there’s always another player on the other side and they're having fun doing their little combo, crushing you, and then it just feels awful to be crushed by the boring combo because they’re sitting there playing solitaire and you're like, "Alright, well..."

**[03:52] David Heron:** Yeah, exactly.

**[03:53] Anthony Giovannetti:** But I don't have to worry about that in *Slay the Spire*. If you reach that kind of stage, you just feel good. The game doesn’t feel bad about, you know, that it’s getting crushed. It’s fine. And so I was aware that this would be okay. And my only thought with, for example, reaching an infinite combo was that I wanted it to, one, I wanted it to be attainable, because that’s a fun thing and that at least some segment of card game players really like that. Right. But I wanted it to be something that you at least had to like work for and wouldn't just, you know, fall into your lap. You had to like strive to get it and work towards it, and maybe be a little difficult, because then you would feel better when you did get it off. Like, "Oh, sweet, I actually, you know, made this sweet little five-card deck that just always does the crazy thing." And then in, you know, most of the runs you don't actually live the dream, but you have that dream you can attain. So that was like the balance I was striving for.

**[04:47] David Heron:** Yeah, that’s an interesting sort of take. I guess it sort of precludes having some sort of multiplayer, but you're right, it is the "I take my turn and then I have to wait 20 minutes." So that’s really good insight, thank you.

**[05:00] Anthony Giovannetti:** Yeah, and in fact, a part—so it’s interesting because I always get a lot of people asking me like, "Oh, when are you going to add multiplayer to *Slay the Spire*?" And the answer I always inevitably give is like, *Slay the Spire*, part of why it works is that it’s a single-player game. And if I was to add multiplayer, I'd basically be designing an entirely different game. Because when I sat down to think about *Slay the Spire*, single-player was like the big thing that’s like not really out there. It’s like, if I just tried to make a card game that was like a competitor to *Hearthstone* or something, I would just go into the sea of clones and, you know, no one would care. But by targeting the single-player niche—like there wasn't really a big hit in that, like at all, right? So that was a space that was kind of empty that we could lodge ourselves in.

**[05:48] Dirk Knemeyer:** When a player’s run ends, new cards are unlocked that increase the universe of available cards for subsequent runs. What were the design reasons for this particular mechanic?

**[05:58] Anthony Giovannetti:** So we actually have two different reasons for this mechanic. One is that we wanted to gate a couple of more complex things behind just like a very simple unlock. So that like when you start with a character, maybe we'll have a really complex mechanic behind some of the unlocks just to hide it so you can get the feel for it first, and some of the more complex relics too. So one, we’re doing that. And then two is we’re giving new players kind of a reason to keep playing after they lose. So like our expectation is that you play your first, you know, your first few runs of *Slay the Spire* and you’re probably not going to do super well. You’re probably going to lose. The unlocks kind of give you a path forward. You’re like, "Oh, okay, I can see I’m still making some progress here." But we actually really don't like to have grindy meta-game systems where you like need to do, you know, a bunch of grinding to like add stats or whatever to your characters like some rogue-lites do. So we wanted to avoid that. So actually like our unlock system is fairly shallow in terms of it doesn’t take very long to unlock all of the cards for a character. And that’s by design. We’re mainly just trying to like get you kind of in the door, and then once you play a little bit to unlock everything, then you’re hooked and you want to just keep playing the game. So we’re not using our unlocks as like "this is why you play," it’s more of a way to like trick people into getting engaged with the core systems and the mechanics.

**[07:23] David Heron:** Yeah, I think it even like—it sort of builds on the idea that the characters themselves—if you take a character you can sort of look at core builds, right? You can take the Silent and you’ve got shivs, you’ve got poison, and they're these like distinct types of builds, or orbs for instance for the Defect. When you look at sort of like other card games and other drafting games, you see similar things, right? There’s certain *Magic* cycles where, you know, there are a bunch of like these decks that require a bunch of synergies—get as many pirates as you can, get as many merfolk as you can. And I think it’s really good in some ways because that sort of signals, "Hey, if you take all these, if you take all the shiv cards, if you take all the poison cards and you put them together, you draft those, you're going to have a deck that works." The side effect I’ve witnessed, though, is like in a *Magic* drafting scenario, it might mean that like that first pack is like super important because if you start down the path of poison, if you start down the path of shivs, it basically dictates, right? Like the first time you pick that powerful card, you say, "Oh, I’m going to play shiv build this." It’s really hard to pivot. Was that like—did you try to do anything with—was there ever a moment where you said like, "mm, maybe we shouldn't be doing this? Maybe we should be allowing for more generic, less synergistic decks?" Was that a thing that mattered?

**[08:44] Anthony Giovannetti:** So there’s actually a couple of things I’ll address here because this is interesting. So if you look at actually like the top-level players—so like the people who are win-streaking Ascension level 20—they play very non-archetype focused. Right, they just—it's optimized. They just want the best cards, yeah. Exactly. So they're actually being more consistently like generic and flexible. So there’s this interesting like inversion where you expect that actually just going super hard on the synergy is the best, but it’s actually slightly non-optimal. It’s a direction that we give people who maybe don't know where they would go. Like, you know, if you have no idea, you can see "Oh, here’s Catalyst, it doubles the poison on an enemy." And if you have—if you’re new to card games, you can look at that card and that card makes you go, "Okay, I can see how I can work with this." And that is super important because not everyone who plays *Slay the Spire* has been playing *Magic* for years like I have. Right, exactly.

And so when we sat down to design the cards for the characters, we had a rule where like we wanted a certain number of build-around-me cards where cards that right when you see the card, your brain can start thinking up strategies and ways to work with it. And we wanted to have lots of those and have these archetypes that players could work towards, but we also wanted to make it, like I said, where, you know, at—if you’re really good at the game, you don't need it.

The other thing is in terms of the first few cards determining like your choice for, especially for people who are just like focusing on the archetypes—I actually really like that property. And so the reason for that is one of the goals with *Slay the Spire* is I wanted to encourage people to be doing different builds and be building different decks basically every time they play, right? So if we’re kind of like leveraging into our strength where because we’re a roguelike, because we’re a single-player game, every time you play you're not going to be seeing the same cards. You can't just be like, "Okay, this is the combo deck that I build and I’m just going to play it every time." Because if we did have that—in fact like some players very early on requested like the ability to choose the starting cards in their deck—but that would actually lead to a very stale gameplay where you’re just like, "Okay, every—I’ve mastered the game, I’ve figured out the one deck and I just play that every time" and then you’re bored. But for example, in like *Magic* or *Hearthstone*, I always enjoyed the drafting or the arena stuff a lot more. That’s where the players picking the cards to add to your deck kind of like you do in *Slay the Spire*. And those—I always found those systems a lot more interesting and that had a lot more replay value to me. And so that was what I wanted to like really hone in on for *Slay the Spire*.

**[11:37] David Heron:** So like—I got a follow-up question because I’m interested about this idea of like—when you laid out "these are the cards that we’re going to build." Because it’s very, very similar to sort of how I worked when we were laying out sets for *Five Rings*. One of the things that was sort of like controversial depending on which sort of part of the designer was the idea that you wanted to put these sorts of themes in like silos. Like this idea that there wouldn't be cross-pollination. But I think in *Slay the Spire*, there’s definitely some cross-pollination. So like if we like go back to like shiv, one of the other sort of like archetypes for the Silent is like—I would say like discard, that sort of discard. And there’s a couple of cards—I forgot the name—where you like discard your whole hand and you get that many shivs. And like—that’s like the secret overpowered card in that deck, you know, because it just like super—once you get a certain number of relics that like trigger off the discard—like it has this multiplicative effect. Was that part of the grand plan when you looked at the spreadsheet and you said, "This is—we’re going to have one shiv discard," or did that just sort of happen naturally?

**[12:47] Anthony Giovannetti:** That was intentional. So we wanted overlap because—so we would kind of classify cards under different like—so we have a very limited card set for each character. It’s like every character gets 75 cards. And so we would kind of think—when we would create a card we’d kind of think about what role it’s performing for the character. So is this card going to be a card that like, you know, shows the player a strategy? Is it going to be a card that’s just like a staple card that, you know, is like a staple damage-dealing card that you can kind of slot into any deck? Or—and we tried to design some cards that actually had that strategy overlap because again, you know, we want to give players tools so they don't just have to be like "I’m just the poison deck," you know? We want people to be able to be like, "Okay, I’m doing poison but I have this card that makes it so whenever I deal damage I apply poison so I can try to play a bunch of shivs on them and stack poison that way too." And it’s more interesting when you do that because the possibility space just grows. And it makes it so your decks are going to end up looking more varied between each other. And I think that’s just a good thing.

**[13:58] Dirk Knemeyer:** You recently gave some really well-received talk at GDC and one of the things you said is that your balance focus was not on cards having equal power, but on each card having its place. Talk more about your design reasons for this balance.

**[14:14] Anthony Giovannetti:** Sure. So there were a couple of reasons for this. One is that really we’re just two people in terms of designers—it’s me and Casey—and we’re balancing like hundreds of cards with hundreds of relics, you know, across just a ton of content. And realistically, I know that it’s impossible to achieve like this kind of platonic state of balance where everything is equal and like—that’s a total dream that like no card game has ever come close to achieving, right? So I knew just out of the gate that like that’s not going to be possible. So what is something that we can work towards? So what I figured is that really what we care about is that I wanted it so that there’s not cards in the game that you just look at and you’re like, "I never have a reason to take this." So what we want is that, okay, it’s okay that some cards can be better than other cards, but as long as you're still going to take a given card some percentage of the time, it’s okay. Like, it has a reason to exist in the game even if players are taking it less. So, you know, maybe there’s a niche card that is very fun to play with and like really fleshes out one strategy, but you don't see that strategy very often. But because sometimes the player’s going to take it and then like really enjoy it when they do, that card still has merit to exist.

And if that—because that was our goal—that was something we could actually like reasonably do. And then also I think it’s a little bit more interesting because, you know, there is skill in the card-picking choice, you know, at the end of every combat when you pick between one of three cards. So it’s fine for the power levels of things to vary a little there. The big thing is that we want to avoid like super dominating overpowered cards that kind of just like warp everything, so where you see it you pick it like 100% of the time, it makes for non-choices. That can be pretty bad. You know, you could have like maybe one rare card in that like for each character that you're like, "Oh, it’s this, you know, it’s like Adrenaline, I always want to pick this." Like that’s fine, but if you have a lot of that, then the game gets stale. It’s ultimately about, for me, it’s more about the like card picking, because that’s kind of like the essence of *Slay the Spire* is in every—at the end of every combat you add a card to your deck. And it’s like that decision matters so much into making the game work. So we really want that decision to be kind of rich and interesting.

**[16:47] Dirk Knemeyer:** Makes a lot of sense. Another thing you said at GDC is that overpowered cards are fine in a roguelike. Why is that? Why in this specific genre are overpowered cards okay?

**[16:59] Anthony Giovannetti:** Sure. So overpowered cards are fine with the caveat that they're not too warping like I said. And the reason I think this is because as I mentioned earlier, it’s single-player. So if I play a card that’s too good, your opponent doesn’t just complain about "Oh, the devs can't balance the game," you know, and you know, they're not getting sour grapes, you’re just having fun feeling powerful over the game. Kind of like in *Diablo*, you know, if you get a really sweet piece of gear or something, you feel good about that moment like when you're crushing the game with it. And then two, because it’s a roguelike, you’re not always going to see that card. So that’s where it comes back to that deck diversity and seeing different things. If you have an overpowered card in *Magic*, everyone slots them into their deck, everyone sees it, the meta-game becomes about that deck, right? And then it’s just like very stale gameplay that’s always the same. In a roguelike, if you have a strong card, you're not always going to see it. Sometimes the thing that you need to like combo with that card might not come up, so you’ll get it and it’s just okay but it’s not like reaching the true depths of like absurdity because you didn't also get the other thing. And so, you know, maybe like it’s only one in 20 runs that you get that cool overpowered combo together. And then so instead of having every single game feel stale, one out of 20 runs you feel really sweet and strong. So it’s like a totally different dynamic in terms of player feel.

**[28:34] Anthony Giovannetti:** Sure, so this is going to sound kind of dumb, but when we just created our—so when we launched under Early Access, we were like, "You know what, we want to be really open with our community, so what we’re going to do is we’re going to create a roadmap and just like say all the things we want to have in 1.0."
