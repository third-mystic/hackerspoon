
# The Craft of Designing Systems in Games

## Author: Ezaz Shaikh
Gameplay & Systems Designer | India GDC of the Year Nominee
February 23, 2026

## Complexity is the Inventory

What players experience as "fun" or "friction" is often the result of long-term structural decisions they never see. Designing these game systems is one of the most misunderstood crafts in our industry because it is frequently mistaken for simply managing complexity. But complexity is not the goal. Depth is.

Before we look at why some games fail while others thrive, we have to understand what a game system actually is. At its highest level, it is the invisible framework: the overarching structure of interacting rules, variables, and feedback loops that determine what happens when mechanics collide.

Within that framework, individual subsystems, such as progression, economy, or combat, influence one another, amplifying or distorting player choices over time. Depth results from this expansion of meaningful decision space, giving players room to experiment and adapt.

A game can have dozens of mechanics, layers of progression, branching upgrades, currencies, modifiers, and edge cases, and still feel shallow. We've seen live-service titles launch with many systems yet fail to retain players because those systems never compound meaningfully, for example, Payday 3 or Suicide Squad: Kill the Justice League.

Sometimes it's not just about the systems. Even well-built systems cannot save a game if it fails to differentiate itself in an exhausted ecosystem, a lesson recently highlighted by Concord's entry into the market.

While massive scale and trend-chasing often lead to these kinds of failures, true systemic depth doesn't require a massive footprint. Some games can operate on a relatively small rule set and support hundreds of hours of meaningful engagement if done correctly.

*Hades* is a prime example of this. It operates on a relatively contained core combat loop, yet the interaction between boons, weapon aspects, and enemy patterns creates extraordinary depth over repeated runs. The difference isn't the amount of complexity. It's what that complexity produces. Complexity is inventory. Depth is experience.

## Where Systems Begin

While systems are large, invisible frameworks, they are powered by the "Core Loop", the repetitive cycle of player actions that drives the experience forward.

A healthy loop follows a reliable cadence: Action - Feedback - Reward - Expansion. For example: You kill an enemy (Action), see a satisfying damage number (Feedback), collect gold (Reward) and use that gold to upgrade your weapon (Expansion). This is one pass through the systemic machine. When designed correctly, each completion of the loop feeds into a larger system, where today's gold becomes tomorrow's economy, today's encounter becomes next week's progression.

If the loop is engaging, players are naturally pulled into the deeper systems. However, if the loop is broken, if the feedback is mushy or the reward doesn't lead to meaningful expansion, no amount of high-level architectural depth can save the game. The friction at the micro-level creates a "cognitive wall," preventing players from ever reaching the macro-level.

Mastery of the craft begins here: ensuring the second-to-second experience is strong enough to carry the weight of the minute-to-minute, the hour-to-hour, and so on.

## Elegance as Structural Clarity

When we talk about complex games, we're usually referring to the sum total of systems, rules, variables, and their possible interactions. But none of that guarantees that players will actually experience richness. Depth happens when players can meaningfully engage with those systems, understand them well enough to form a mental model, and then explore the consequences of their decisions over time.

*Baldur's Gate 3* is complex, but its turn structure, readable tooltips, and clear cause-and-effect combat feedback allow players to reason about outcomes. The transformation from complexity to depth is not automatic. That transformation depends on structural clarity.

Elegance in systems design is often misunderstood as polish or aesthetic minimalism. In reality, elegance is structural clarity. It determines whether players can cross the entry barrier of understanding. A complex system without clarity becomes cognitive load. The same system, presented with readable feedback and coherent staging, becomes an invitation to explore.

*Persona 5 Royal*, for example. It is a structurally elegant game that manages to layer social systems, dungeon mechanics, turn-based combat, and daily scheduling without collapsing into chaos. But despite this clarity, it highlights a crucial reality: elegance does not erase the entry barrier, it only frames it. For a JRPG veteran, that layering feels gradual and inviting. For newcomers, the onboarding sequence can still feel overwhelming. The systems themselves are not inherently inaccessible. The perceived entry cost is.

This is where many games struggle. They may not lack depth. They may lack the clarity that allows players to reach it. If the entry cost feels opaque, players disengage before they ever experience the reward for crossing the entry barrier.

That entry barrier, however, is not fixed. It varies by player, genre, and expectations. A veteran will parse layered mechanics as gradual onboarding. A newcomer might experience the same sequence as an avalanche of rules. The systems haven't changed. The starting point has.

This variability makes tutorial and onboarding design extraordinarily difficult. Teaching a system is not just about explaining rules. It's about calibrating exposure. What do you introduce first? What can remain optional? What can remain invisible until later? What complexity must be understood now, and what can safely wait?

*Genshin Impact*, for instance, allows players to enjoy its world and Some games manage this brilliantly by separating the system's actual complexity from the required complexity to play. They establish a remarkably low skill floor while preserving a massive skill ceiling. Early on, encounters can often be brute-forced using basic intuition or simple mechanics. The deeper layers of intricate synergies, resource management, or min-maxing stats exist from minute one, but are not mandatory for early success. Players can begin engaging, find a hook, and then gradually choose to invest more cognitive effort as the game slowly dials up the friction. The barrier is not removed, rather, it is simply placed further down the road, exactly when the player is motivated to cross it.

## Depth Across Genres

When players finally cross that threshold and reach the core of the game, what exactly do they find? The answer varies because depth itself does not look the same across genres. In some games, depth emerges primarily from interacting rules and expanding decision space. Strategy RPGs, card battlers, and simulation games derive their long-term engagement from combinatorial systems and compounding consequences.

*Slay the Spire* is a clear example of this. Its core mechanics are simple: draw cards, spend energy and play cards, manage health, and defeat enemies. But the interaction between relics, card synergies, enemy intents, and deck composition creates exponential decision depth. The ruleset is readable. The feedback is transparent. The depth emerges not from adding more systems, but from how those systems intersect.

In other genres, depth emerges from the refinement of feel, feedback, and the sense of impending mastery. In other words, the depth stems from how well the fantasy is translated into gameplay.

*Sea of Thieves* demonstrates this perfectly. Its underlying mechanical systems are relatively straightforward, but the emergent player interactions, shared world risk, naval handling, and social unpredictability create long-term engagement. The depth does not primarily come from layered stat systems or progression trees, but from how players inhabit and test the space together.

The systems are still there, but the player's experience of depth is more experiential than combinatorial. Both are valid. Both are systemic. The manifestation differs.

However, these different depths are not arbitrary, they are designed to satisfy different psychological needs. To understand why one player loses themselves in a spreadsheet-heavy strategy game while another finds depth in the unpredictability of a shared ocean, we have to look at the "why" behind the engagement.

## Psychology of Engagement

Understanding the structure of a game is essential, but a system must ultimately align with human psychology to feel meaningful. Systems designers must choose which motivational hooks to lean on: extrinsic rewards or intrinsic mastery.

In systems focused on extrinsic rewards, the motivation is provided externally via a constant stream of "carrots."

Conversely, others like *Sekiro* lean into intrinsic mastery, the satisfaction of internalizing a complex system and performing it perfectly. In these games, the reward is the player's own perceived growth in skill.

A great system designer knows how to balance two. If a game relies solely on extrinsic rewards, it risks becoming a "treadmill" where players stop having fun the moment the loot stops flowing. Conversely, if it relies only on intrinsic mastery, the entry cost can feel too steep for those who aren't yet in.

The goal is to create a "Sense of Agency". Depth is only rewarding if the player feels their choices matter. When a system allows a player to say, "I succeeded because I understood the rules better than the game expected," you have achieved the highest form of engagement. You aren't just managing a player's time, you are facilitating their growth.

But facilitating that growth is a double-edged sword. As players grow and their mastery deepens, they begin to use the systems in ways that were impossible to execute on day one. This creates a massive gap between a designer's vision and the reality of a live environment.

## Danger of Delayed Feedback

Understanding the shape of a game's depth in theory is very different from validating it in practice. This brings us to one of the hardest parts of systems design: the delay in feedback. Designing thirty seconds of fun gives you immediate signals. Designing systems intended to support three hundred hours does not. The consequences of structural decisions often emerge months later, sometimes after launch, when players begin pushing against the edges in ways you didn't anticipate.

One such extreme example is *Diablo III's* auction house. Upon release, it dramatically altered loot perception and progression pacing in ways that only became fully apparent once players optimized around it. It fundamentally undermined the game's core "loot hunt" design, making purchasing items more efficient than finding them. It caused severe player dissatisfaction by forcing players to rely on real-money or gold transactions for progression. Hence, it had to be taken down.

This is why systems design cannot rely purely on intuition. Intuition is an indicator, not a foundation. It can help flag areas of interest, highlight friction, or suggest where something feels off. But long-term systems require theory, abstraction, and disciplined testing, such as paper playtesting, spreadsheet modeling, and simulations, as well as early UX validation before the system fully exists.

Good systems designers think in consequences. They visualize compounding interactions. They consider how players will exploit edges, how economies might inflate, how progression curves might compress or stretch, and most importantly, how each shift alters the available decision space. They anticipate failure modes and build in resilience.

## Math, Pacing, and Balance

Beneath the high-level philosophy of systems design lies a more disciplined, quantitative reality: the math. The craft of shaping a system often happens in spreadsheets, where designers model progression curves, economic "sinks and sources," and drop rates. This is where the abstract concept of "depth" is translated into raw data.

Balancing is the art of ensuring that variables such as damage output, resource costs, and currency inflation stay within the "Goldilocks zone."

Consider the "WoW Token" crisis in *World of Warcraft*. When Blizzard announced players could convert in-game gold into real-world Battle.net balance, they didn't just change a menu option, but rewired the entire economy. The demand for tokens skyrocketed, causing gold prices to double overnight as players rushed to liquidate years of hoarded wealth. It was a stark reminder that if the "sources" of gold (loot/quests) aren't perfectly balanced against "sinks" (token costs/mounts), the perceived value of a player's time can collapse in hours.

To prevent these types of systemic collapses, designers use quantitative "stress tests" to probe the game's internal logic before it ever reaches the public. By running simulations or "Monte Carlo" methods, designers can see how a system might look after 100 hours of play. Will the player be a billionaire with nothing to buy? Will the combat math break at level 50? Mastering the quantitative craft means ensuring that the game's internal logic remains robust, fair, and rewarding from the first minute to the hundredth hour.

## Load-Bearing Pillars

Good systems design is not about predicting perfectly. It is about designing structures that can absorb imbalance without collapsing. When something shifts, be it player behavior, meta strategies, or even the content cadence, the system should bend before it breaks.

Architectural metaphors are often used for systems design, and for good reason. You need to know which pillars are decorative and which are load-bearing. But unlike physical architecture, game systems reveal their structural weaknesses only when players inhabit them. Some pillars only appear load-bearing. ## Inevitability of Trade-Offs

Once the structure is resilient, you have to decide what it is optimized for. This brings us to a harsh reality: there is no such thing as a perfect system that pleases everyone. Every decision in systems design solves one problem and creates another.

In development, there is often pressure to "have it all", to build a system that is infinitely deep for veterans but instantly frictionless for beginners. But true design requires making a definitive choice of what you are willing to sacrifice. Push clarity too far, and you risk flattening depth. Push depth too far, and you risk alienating newcomers. Push monetization, and you may reduce the satisfaction of mastery. Push accessibility, and you may reduce the satisfaction of mastery.

The industry saw exactly what happens when that monetization balance fails during the launch of *Star Wars Battlefront II*. By tying progression and hero unlocks to loot boxes, the game effectively intertwined monetization with power. What was meant to accelerate rewards ended up distorting fairness and agency. The resulting outrage led to regulatory scrutiny, massive PR fallout, and a complete redesign of the progression model.

Navigating these kinds of minefields is exactly what defines the role. The work is not about eliminating trade-offs. It is about choosing the right ones. This is also why system design mastery is rare. It demands the ability to think long-term in an industry often driven by short-term metrics. It requires tolerance for delayed feedback. It demands comfort with abstraction and humility in the face of emergent behavior. It asks you to balance theory, testing, intuition, and restraint, all while committing to structures that may be expensive to change later.

## True Craft of Systems Design

When systems fail, it is rarely because they lacked ideas. More often, they lacked clarity, resilience, or alignment with player expectations. Ultimately, a game's systemic architecture will perfectly produce the results it is structured to deliver, regardless of the designer's intent. If players bounce before reaching the depth the designer intended, that is not a failure on the player's part. That outcome is a structural consequence of the design.

"Systems design, at its core, is not about making things complex. It is about shaping consequences over time. It is about carving out a space where players can explore, experiment, and invest. It is about ensuring that the cost of entry is justified by the richness that lies beyond it. Ultimately, complexity is the inventory, depth is the experience, and the true craft of systems design is transforming one into the other."
